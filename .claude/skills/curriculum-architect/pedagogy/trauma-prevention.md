# Trauma Prevention Protocols

This document defines safety protocols to prevent psychological trauma during swim instruction. A single traumatic experience can set a student back weeks or cause them to quit entirely.

## The Cost of Trauma

When a student has a traumatic experience in water:
- **Immediate**: Panic, hyperventilation, loss of trust in coach
- **Short-term**: Reluctance to return, regression of skills, increased anxiety
- **Long-term**: May never learn to swim, may pass fear to their children

**Prevention is mandatory**. There is no acceptable level of student trauma.

---

## High-Risk Situations

### 1. Deep Water Without Preparation
**Risk**: Student gets to deep end, can't touch bottom, panics.

**Prevention**:
- Always assess comfort with depth BEFORE swimming lengths
- Use Joseph's depth desensitization: tap bottom, stand flat, release wall
- Never let a student swim away from where they can stand until they've demonstrated recovery

### 2. Equipment Failure
**Risk**: Noodle slips, kickboard is dropped, student loses support unexpectedly.

**Prevention**:
- Teach students that equipment is for confidence, not survival
- Practice "what if noodle slips" scenarios while supervised
- Always have backup equipment within reach

### 3. Stopping at the Deep End
**Risk**: Student swims to deep end, gets tired, can't continue or return.

**Prevention**:
- **Never let struggling students stop at the deep end**
- Use noodle under hips for students who might not make it back
- Position coach between student and deep end when possible
- Teach "turn and push off" before swimming full lengths

### 4. Water Ingestion
**Risk**: Student accidentally inhales water, chokes, panics.

**Prevention**:
- Teach breath control before any face-in-water activities
- Always breathe OUT when face is in water (prevents inhalation)
- Practice "if you swallow water, cough it out, you're fine" scenarios
- Normalize the experience: "Everyone swallows water sometimes"

### 5. Fatigue-Induced Panic
**Risk**: Student gets tired, form breaks down, starts struggling.

**Prevention**:
- Watch for early fatigue signs: slowing pace, higher breathing rate, splashier movements
- Call students back BEFORE they get exhausted
- "Let's take a break" is always acceptable
- Never push a student to "just finish the length"

## Warning Signs of Impending Panic

### Physical Signs
- **Wide eyes** (whites visible all around iris)
- **Thrashing** instead of swimming
- **Grabbing** for anything (wall, lane rope, coach)
- **Vertical body position** (trying to climb out of water)
- **Gasping** instead of controlled breathing
- **Sudden stillness** (freeze response)

### Verbal Signs
- "I can't do this"
- "Help"
- Inability to respond to questions
- Screaming or crying

### Behavioral Signs
- Stopping mid-activity
- Moving toward wall rapidly
- Looking for exit
- Ignoring coach instructions

---

## Intervention Protocol

### Level 1: Early Signs (Mild Anxiety)
**What you see**: Hesitation, nervous laughter, frequent wall touching

**Response**:
1. Move closer to student
2. Offer reassurance: "You're doing fine. I'm right here."
3. Reduce task difficulty: "Let's just float for a moment"
4. Give control back: "Do you want to try again or rest?"

### Level 2: Escalating Anxiety
**What you see**: Rapid breathing, gripping wall, refusing to let go

**Response**:
1. Get within arm's reach
2. Calm, slow voice: "I've got you. You're safe."
3. Physical anchor if needed (hand on shoulder)
4. Stop all instruction
5. Simple commands: "Feet down. Stand up. Good."
6. Rest until breathing normalizes

### Level 3: Active Panic
**What you see**: Thrashing, grabbing, screaming, vertical position

**Response**:
1. **Rescue position**: Get behind student to avoid being grabbed
2. Support under arms from behind
3. "I've got you. Put your feet down."
4. Move to wall or shallow area
5. **Do not let go until student is stable**
6. Exit pool if needed
7. Session is over for this student—no more instruction today

### Post-Incident Protocol
After any Level 2 or 3 event:
1. **Debrief privately**: "That was scary. Let's talk about what happened."
2. **Validate**: "Your reaction was normal. Water can be scary."
3. **Explain**: "Here's what we'll do differently next time."
4. **Agency**: "You're in control. We only do what you're ready for."
5. **Document**: Record incident in student notes
6. **Follow up**: Check in before next session

## Environmental Safety

### Pool Assessment
Before every session, check:
- Depth markers visible and accurate
- No obstacles in swimming path
- Lane ropes in place if needed
- Rescue equipment accessible (ring, pole)
- Exit points clear

### Crowded Pool Protocol (Lagos reality)
Saturday mornings get crowded. Adjustments:
- Start early (before 10:30 AM)
- Use designated lanes if available
- Brief students on lane sharing etiquette
- Maintain closer supervision
- Reduce group size if needed

### Weather Considerations
- No outdoor instruction during lightning
- Wind can create waves—adjust expectations
- Cold water increases fatigue—shorten sessions

---

## Coach Positioning

### During Skill Instruction
- Position between student and deep water
- Within arm's reach for new skills
- Clear sightlines to all students in group

### During Distance Swimming
- Walk alongside student on pool deck
- At deep end, ready to assist
- Never turn back on a swimming student

### Group Sessions
- Maximum 1:6 ratio for beginners
- All students visible at all times
- Assistant coach for groups of 4+

## Equipment as Confidence (Not Crutch)

### The Noodle Philosophy
Noodles are for **psychological confidence**, not physical support.

**Right framing**: "This noodle helps you feel secure while we practice. You could swim without it, but it lets you focus on technique."

**Wrong framing**: "Hold onto this so you don't sink."

### When to Remove Equipment
Remove equipment when:
1. Student requests it
2. Student demonstrates skill without relying on it
3. Equipment is preventing proper technique

Never remove equipment:
1. As punishment
2. To "challenge" an unwilling student
3. Without warning

**Transition protocol**:
1. "Let's try one without the noodle. I'll be right here."
2. Stay within reach
3. Celebrate success: "See? You did that yourself."
4. Give choice: "Do you want to keep going without it?"

## The "Big Pool First" Philosophy

Counter-intuitively, starting in the deep pool can PREVENT trauma.

### Why It Works
- Conquering the scary thing first builds massive confidence
- 6 feet becomes the baseline—4 feet feels trivial after
- Students learn depth is manageable with proper skills
- No surprise "it gets deep here" moments later

### How to Implement
1. Start at wall in deep section
2. Wall holds: "See? The wall is right here."
3. Bottom tapping: "The bottom is there. You can reach it."
4. Standing: "You can stand on the bottom and still hold the wall."
5. Release: "Now let go for 3 seconds. I'm right here."

### Who This Doesn't Work For
- Students with severe phobia (start in shallow, graduate to deep)
- Students who can't touch the bottom even slightly
- Students with physical limitations

## Trauma Recovery Protocol

If a student has had a traumatic experience (with you or previous coach):

### Session 1: Trust Rebuilding
- No instruction, just conversation
- "What happened before?"
- "What scared you most?"
- "What would help you feel safe?"
- End with one tiny success (even just putting feet in)

### Session 2: Micro-Progressions
- Start below their comfort zone
- "We're going to do things you CAN do, not things you can't yet"
- Celebrate every small win
- Stop before they want to stop (leave them wanting more)

### Ongoing
- Check in frequently: "How are you feeling?"
- Permission to quit any time: "Say the word and we stop"
- Progress at THEIR pace, not curriculum pace
- Document what works and what triggers anxiety

---

## Red Lines (Never Do These)

1. **Never push a student underwater** (even playfully)
2. **Never remove support equipment without consent**
3. **Never leave a struggling student** to "figure it out"
4. **Never mock or minimize fear** ("It's just water")
5. **Never continue instruction during panic** (safety first, teaching second)
6. **Never let students swim beyond their recovery capability**
7. **Never prioritize curriculum completion over student wellbeing**

---

## Reporting and Documentation

### Incident Report Required For
- Any Level 3 panic event
- Any physical rescue needed
- Any student injury
- Any student who quits due to fear

### Documentation Includes
- Date, time, location
- What happened (factual)
- Student's state before, during, after
- Coach response
- Follow-up plan
- Student/guardian notified (if applicable)

### Review Process
- All incidents reviewed by program director
- Patterns identified across students/coaches
- Training updated based on learnings
